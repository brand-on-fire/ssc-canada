The following article describes how to view the secondary school rankings of the Fraser Institute in Canada for reference.

Special thanks to: Kai Tak Guangzhou for studying abroad 2015-11-24

Chinese people attach great importance to children’s education. With the increase in immigrants, more parents have set their sights on studying in Canadian middle schools. However, it is difficult for parents to decide whether to choose a public school or private school. Many parents will visit the Fraser Institute’s secondary school rankings to help make the decision.

Here are some important questions to ask:

Is the top school friendly for international students?

How do I interpret the report’s middle school rankings?

Here is more information in how to Analyze the Fraser Institute’s Secondary School Rankings in BC:

The annual ranking of BC secondary schools published by the Fraser Institute has great reference value for selecting schools. However, if you simply look at the rankings, you will find that public schools are not as good as private schools.  However, from the perspective of cultivating talented people, public schools and private schools are equal and neither one has special advantages.  Each year, outstanding graduates from public schools and private schools are enrolled in the top universities in the world.  Successful students are found throughout a wide variety of public and private schools, and they are not concentrated in just a few private schools.

In fact, the Fraser Institute has an excellent mix of public schools and private schools in it’s rankings, but many high quality public schools appear lower on the ranking.  The Fraser Institute uses the following indicators to weight the average rankings:  Average exam grades, percentage of exams failed, school vs exam grades difference, gender gaps in English and Math, graduation rate, and delayed advancement rate.

First, the number of students in each grade of the public school is far more than the number of students in private schools, and some are four times or more than five times that of private schools.  For example, the 12th grade in Lord Byng public school registered 261 people, Price of Wales 205, Handsworth 270, Elgin Park 271, Magee 229, Point Grey 279.  In comparison, the private school York House has 12th grade registrations for 54 people, 145 for St George, 62 for South Ridge, 60 for West Point Grey, 81 for Crofton House, and 94 for Collingwood. Therefore, when calculating the Average exam mark and percentage of exams failed, which are the main factors of ranking, it is quite unfavorable for the public school.

Secondly, the other two unfavorable ranking factors for public schools are the so-called Delayed Advancement Rate (10, 11 and 12 years combined rate of repetition, showing that the school is unable to complete the last three years of schooling as expected) and graduation. The rate (Graduate rate). For public schools, because students cannot pass the examinations, the student level is uneven. With a large number of people, it is almost inevitable that someone cannot graduate or repeat. In addition, new immigrants have just arrived in Canada and their English is not good enough. Transfers of school leave and immigration backflow all affect the graduation rate and repetition rate data. For example, Lord Byng’s graduation rate and repetition rate were 99.1% and 8.1%, Prince of Wales was 98% and 8.9%, Hands worth was 98.5% and 6.5%, Elgin Park was 97.2% and 7.8%, and Magee was 98% and 7.4%, Point Grey were 95% and 18.4%, and Sir Winston Churchill was 96.8% and 15.2%. In contrast to private schools, because students are relatively stable in their semester, the graduation rate is basically 100%, and the repetition rate is almost zero. For some private schools, student retention means “taking away.” Comparing these two indicators, it is impossible for a public school that cannot select students to select a large number of students to be a rival of a private school.

Obviously, the use of the above indicators provides mixed rankings of public schools and private schools.  However, this does not mean that private schools are better than public schools. We can imagine that if Lord Byng is allowed to leave only its top 60 students, its ranking is almost certainly the number one in BC. By extension, if traditional elite schools such as Prince of Wales, Hands worth, Rock ridge, Sentinel, Elgin Park, Magee, Point Grey and West Vancouver are allowed to retain only their best 60 students to participate in the competition, then the rankings will also be skewed.

Looking at Sand Institute will offer another perspective.  In this case, how can we use the ranking of the Fraser Institute to better understand the quality of the school? After all, this is the result of the experts’ research.  Solution: Separate the public schools and private schools and look at independent rankings.   This will show the relative fairness of each school’s strength.  Parents of students can also choose according to the orientation of private schools in public schools.  If we must mix public and private schools, I am afraid we can only change the method of assessment.  Otherwise, it is difficult to make a relatively fair comparison of the standards of public and private schools.

SSC-Canada Perspective

How do you see how to look at the Fraser Institute ‘s high school rankings ? SSC-Canada’s view point is:  Use it for basic reference, rather than absolute definitive answer! For parents to choose the best school for their children, it is important not only to look at the school rankings, but also to obtain a clear understanding of the school’s quality itself.
